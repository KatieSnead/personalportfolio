Summary

Individuals currently need multiple storage options, depending on the content; Katalogus provides a “one-stop-shop” solution that everyone can get behind! Katalogus is a cloud storage web application focused on the ability to organize and share all your content, web and personal, in one secure place.

Problem

Currently, client clients are typically having to go to PlatformX for documents, PlatformY for pictures and PlatformZ for links (articles / recipes / etc). This is frustrating and hard to manage.

Solution

Katalogus is your only cloud platform that has the capability of storing files of all types: PDF, word documents, pictures, website links, and more! Plus, by keeping the platform simple in its interface and similar to other platforms, customers will have an easy time taking to jump to a new cloud service.

Discovery & Research

Competitive Analysis

I was very worried that there would really be no true solution to bringing in another player to the cloud storage marketplace. It is already saturated with big-hitters that can easily sweep a newcomer underwater. However, I was quick to find out that there are some holes in each of the platforms.

What iCloud does best is being able to be used virtually in the background. Content is saved to individuals’ phone or computer, which is auto-pushed to the cloud.
Where iCloud falls short is the lack of full-collaboration capabilities between Apple and non-Apple users.
What Google Drive does best (beyond having a strong parent company reputation to back it) is collaboration.
Their interface can feel clunky and there are often formatting issues from uploaded items.
Pictures / articles can be saved from anywhere on the web and easily organized into personalized folders
Pinterest is primarily known for saving content found on the web, so it is easy for users to pigeon-hole the entire platform and not use its other features.

User Research

Through a survey, I was able to see that participants have many different platforms to save content on, depending on the item type; this revealed the hole that current cloud platforms currently have.

Key Insights

59%

‘Screenshot’ digital content to save/ share with others

79%

Often share web content by sending a link to the site

48%

Save website links into word documents to view later

70%

Save PDFs / images from websites into photos folder

77%

Primarily access digital content on their desktop

Analysis

User Personas

After analyzing the survey data, paired with the knowledge gained from the competitive analysis, it was clear that current cloud clients use different platforms for a wide variety of tasks and want their cloud(s) to “keep up” as well as not being required to use multiple platforms for essentially the same task.

User Stories

Keeping Rebecca and Marc in mind, and going off the results from both the survey and the competitive analysis, I crafted the User Stories. The high priority stories were ones that were absolute base-level attributes of the platform that would be required even with all the bells and whistles removed. From there, medium-priority stories were created, defined as not being basic necessities of the platform but stories that still would be driving forces/features behind acquiring and maintaining loyalty.

    As a new user, I want to:
  • Create an account
  • Learn about the site's features and benefits
    As a returning user, I want to:
  • Upload a file from my computer
  • Save an article / recipe / etc. from the internet
  • Share a file with someone

User Flows

With those stories in mind, as well as the insights gained from the user survey and competitive analysis, I planned out flows that would feel native to each individual using the platform. It was important to make sure that each user story had a matching flow and that the flow was intuitive. I spent a lot of time with multiple different competitors to understand their flows and shadow consistent themes.

Visual Design

Wireframes

I started the process of wireframing with sketches to quickly feel out how each flow would look, screen to screen, including multiple alternatives. It was important during this point to also research how different flows were handled in many different apps and websites, including that of competitors, to ensure an intuitive design that was not too foreign. From the sketches, I produced digital wireframes to give a more realistic feel before moving on to usability testing.

Mockups

From the prototype / usability test stage, I moved onto designing the entire platform. Shortly after completing a couple screens, I realized that there was a potential accessibility issue with the color scheme chosen. That was tested on a third party site and subsequently failed.

I used the same general color (maroon/purple) to signify elegance and power but also added in orange for a hint of excitement. With those two being the main colors, I chose complementary colors that aligned with a beautiful sunset.

Julius Sans One was chosen as the primary brand front, with Cormorant as the secondary. These two fonts go well together because they are opposites on virtually every level. However, it was discovered that Cormorant, in smaller sizes, becomes difficult to read. With that knowledge, the font pairing choice was changed to Oswald and Montserrat to maintain hierarchy while also having a similar font style.

Based on the information architecture I had in mind and the feedback received, I followed the minor third typography scale for the landing page and the major second scale for the rest of the platform. The landing page starts off with an attention grabber with the “attention grabbing” size of 67 then working down the scale to 16 at the smallest.

Prototypes & Testing

Usability Testing

    Round One Tasks:
  • 1. Create a new account
  • 2. Add a folder to the cloud
  • 3. Add a tag to a file

During the first round of usability testing, I asked participants to complete three tasks while thinking out loud as well as requesting additional feedback on the landing page and desktop. The tasks were to sign up for an account, add a folder to the cloud and add a tag to a specific file.

Each task was completed successfully by every participant; the only issue with tasks was the participants’ ability to understand how to use the prototype (most wanted to click the button to create the account before actually typing in information) but I did get valuable feedback about the dashboard itself.

One participant noted that she had just signed up for an account and yet there are already a bunch of files loaded in -- this feedback reminded me to include a blank state screen for the dashboard!

    Round Two Tasks:
  • 1. Create a new account
  • 2. Add/Save a URL to their cloud
  • 3. Favorite/Star a folder

During the second round of usability testing, both in-person and remote usability tests were conducted, and the project was submitted to a design critique multiple times to allow for further iterations and improvement.

In the design critique, it was recommended to use a type scale in order to choose the best size combinations, as well as recommended trying out a different font combination since the stroke weight of Cormorant cause legibility issues as it got smaller.

In the usability tests, my color choices were enforced by the participants. They also agreed that some type on the landing page could go larger to be more impactful.

Preference (A/B) Testing

I put my iterations to test by creating multiple A/B tests to assess the usability and preference of design options.

    Designs tested were:
  • 1. Overall color scheme (dark versus light)
  • 2. Background design of above-the-fold area of landing page (solid versus gradient)
  • 3. Styling of mobile log-in screen (full or partial gradient)

The results were very strong, so there was no doubt in my mind how the participants felt -- each of the “winning” designs were implemented long term in the platform.

Branding

Mindmap, Moodboard & Logo

Since a wide variety of individuals were being targeted with this platform, with the focus being saving, organizing, and sharing, I started with a mindmap to see what ideas came off of that. Two words in particular really stood out: catalogue and inventory, which then opened the flood gates for logo ideas.

When considering logos, I mimicked imagery of inventory tags, clouds, data groupings and paperclips. The paperclip idea was what continued -- a sturdy tool to hold together all your paperwork was the perfect idea for a sturdy platform to hold all your online content.

From there, I created a moodboard that displayed pictures of organization as well as companionship. Initially, Melgrim was chosen for the main brand typography, but it is not legible beyond the company name, so that is how it has been chosen to remain. The only time Melgrim is used is with the company name itself.

Conclusion

Final Words

Initially, I was very worried that there would really be no true solution to bringing in another player to the cloud storage marketplace. It is already saturated with big-hitters that can easily sweep a newcomer underwater. However, I was quick to find out that there are some holes in each of the platforms that would allow someone else to come in and succeed.

I focused a lot during my user research on keeping a flow in the survey while also keeping it short and simple. If done again, I would have dropped the idea of questions flowing together, understanding that each question is important in-and-of itself, and asked more well-rounded questions during my user research stage. I still had great information to go off of, but could have had even better research had I taken the time to do so.

I learned a great deal from this project, including the need to test font choices at all scales as well as choosing a color scheme with overlapping colors. Mistakes made early on were that the font choice did not scale well for smaller text and the color scheme looked great side by side, but was inaccessible when it came time to using colors chosen for buttons and other overlapping designs. I learned the importance of learning how to use “components” when designing rather than changing something in each frame when an update was made (yikes, I know).

This project, more than anything, made me painfully aware of both research and properly using the tool at hand. I believe wholeheartedly that research done properly at the front end will significantly improve the product near the back end (including stopping critical errors from being made that completely wastes time and effort!). I also see the power that design tools have and how valuable they can be in making design efficient, when used properly.

I am truly proud of this project all together, with it being my first project completed from start to finish, I am most proud of the persistence I had while working on the mockups. There were many times I was truly stuck, unable to figure out what the next step is. While working completely remote, with no true “team” to turn to, I found and utilized many resources so keep moving and to get assistance and collaboration to continue to build a great product. There were times where I felt like there was no light at the end of the tunnel, but I kept chugging along and now have a completed project that I can proudly put my name on!

I’d also like to thank you for taking the time to read / scroll / skim (whichever!) my case study. Please do not hesitate to reach out with any questions, I am more than happy to discuss any detail of this project.
